The aim of this lecture is to prove Sperner's Lemma, which states that every
proper coloring of a simplicial subdivision of a $d$-dimensional simplex $T$ has
a colorful cell. In fact, we will show a stronger result, which is that every
proper coloring of a simplicial subdivision has an \textit{odd number} of
colorful cells, so there is at least one. 

\section{Sperner's Lemma: The Two-Dimensional Case}
We build up to the proof of Sperner's Lemma by establishing some definitions.
These definitions lead to a proof of the lemma in two-dimensions, which we will
use later in the main proof.

\subsection{Definitions}
\begin{definition}[Simplex]
 For our purposes, a \textit{simplex} is the generalization of a
 triangle to arbitrary dimensions\footnote{See
 \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex}
 {https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex}. The term refers to the fact that the
 simplex is the simplest \textit{polytope} representable in a given dimension.}.
 In zero dimensions, a simplex is a point.  In one dimension a simplex is a
 line segment.  In two dimensions, a simplex is a triangle, and a
 three-dimensional simplex is a tetrahedron.
 \end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Simplicial Subdivision]
  Given a simplex $S$, a \textit{subdivision} of $S$ is a decomposition of $S$
  into smaller simplexes. We require that the union of the simplices is the
  original simplex $S$, and that any two simplices meet along a shared edge, at
  a shared vertex, or not at all (i.e., they are disjoint).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Proper Coloring of a Simplicial Subdivision]
 A \textit{proper coloring} of a subdivision of a simplex $S$ ensures that each
 corner $v_0,v_1,v_2$ of $S$ receives a distinct color $0,1,2$. Each subdivision
 vertex on edge $\{v_i,v_j\}$ must carry color $i$ or $j$. Interior subdivision
 vertices can take any color.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Colorful Triangle]
 A \textit{colorful triangle} in a properly colored simplicial subdivision of a
 simplex $S$ is a triangle whose three vertices carry all three of the colors
 $0,1,2$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Sperner's Lemma in Two Dimensions}
\begin{lemma}[Sperner: Two-Dimensional Case]
  Every properly labeled simplicial subdivision of a $2$-dimensional simplex $S$
  contains an odd number of colorful triangles. 
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  Let $T$ be a properly colored subdivision of a $2$-dimensional simplex $S$.
  Construct a subgraph $G$ of its dual as follows. The nodes of $G$ are the cells of $T$ plus
  one more node for the unbounded face. Stipulate that two nodes of $G$ are
  adjacent if and only if their corresponding cells in $T$ share an edge whose
  endpoints are colored $0$ and $1$.  Then the following color combinations are
  possible: 
  \begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Possible vertex colorings of cells in $T$ and the degree count of
    the node corresponding to that cell in $G$} 
    \label{tab:label}
    \begin{tabular}{l l l}
      Cell vertex coloring & $\{0,1\}$-edges & Degree in $G$\\
      \hline
      $\{0,0,0\},\,\{1,1,1\},\,\{2,2,2\}$ & 0 & 0\\
      $\{0,0,2\},\,\{1,1,2\},\,\{0,2,2\},\,\{1,2,2\}$ & 0 & 0\\
      $\{0,0,1\},\,\{0,1,1\}$ & 2 &2\\
      $\{0,1,2\}$ & 1 &1
    \end{tabular}
  \end{table}
  Since this table lists all possible cases, then among bounded cells, colorful
  triangles are the only cells that correspond to odd-degree nodes in $G$. 
  
  Next, label $W$ as the node in the dual that corresponds to the unbounded
  face of $T$. We claim $W$ has odd degree. Why? The $0,1$ side of $T$ (that is,
  the side of $T$ whose endpoints are colored $0$ and $1$) starts with color
  $0$, alternates between runs of $0$'s and runs of $1$'s, and ends with color
  $1$. Thus, the number of $0 - 1$ and $1 - 0$ switches must be odd. 

  Finally, any graph has an even number of odd-degree nodes by the handshaking
  lemma. Since the degree of $W$ in $G$ is odd, $G$ must contain an odd number
  of colorful triangles.
\end{proof}

\section{Sperner's Lemma in \texorpdfstring{$n$}{n}-Dimensions}
It turns out the lemma holds for higher dimension. Indeed, the lemma is just the
inductive step needed to obtain the higher-dimensional result, which we call
Sperner's Lemma. However, before we can prove it, we need to add to
our list of definitions.

\subsection{Definitions}
\begin{definition}[$d$-Dimensional Simplex]
  A \textit{$d$-dimensional simplex} in $\mathbb{R}^n$ ($d\le n$) is the set of
  convex combinations of $d+1$ points that are not all in one affine
  $(d-1)$-dimensional space.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Simplicial Subdivision in $\mathbb{R}^n$]
  A simplicial subdivision of a $d$-dimensional simplex $S\subset \mathbb{R}^n$
  is a decomposition of $S$ into smaller $d$-dimensional simplices. We call the
  smaller $d$-dimensional simplices \textit{cells}. As in the
  two-dimensional case above, the union of the pieces is $S$, and the
  intersection of any two pieces is a shared face (possibly empty).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Facet]
  For a $d$-dimensional simplex $S$ with corners $w_0,w_1,\ldots,w_d$, a
  \textit{facet} is a $(d-1)$-dimensional simplex generated by $d$ of the
  corners of $S$ (i.e., by all but one).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Proper coloring in $\mathbb{R}^n$]
  A proper coloring of a subdivision of a $d$-dimensional simplex $S$
  is a coloring of the vertices from the colors $0,1,\ldots,d$ such that color
  $i$ does not appear on the $i$th facet of $S$ (that is, the facet that omits
  corner $w_i$).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Colorful Cell]
  A colorful cell is a cell that has all $d+1$ colors $0,1,\ldots,d$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Sperner's Lemma}
\begin{lemma}[Sperner's Lemma]
  Every proper coloring of a simplicial subdivision $T$ of a $d$-dimensional
  simplex $S$ has an odd number of colorful cells. 
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  The proof is by induction on $d$. If $d=0$ the result is immediate. 
  For $d = 2$ we have the 2-dimensional result which we just proved.
  For $d \geq 3$, form a graph $G$ with a node for each cell of $T$ and a node $W$ for
  the unbounded face. Join any two nodes with an edge if their
  corresponding cells in $T$ intersect in a $(d-1)$-dimensional simplex whose
  corners have every color $0,1,\ldots,d-1$, but not color $d$. Every colorful
  cell has degree one, (as argued before), while every other bounded cell has
  degree 0 or degree 2. The vertex $W$ has neighbors in $G$ only through $T'$,
  the subdivision induced by $T$ on the $d$th facet of $S$ (the facet of $S$
  that omits corner $w_d$). Restricting the coloring of $T$ to $T'$ yields a
  proper coloring in $d-1$ dimensions: its completely labeled regions connect
  to $W$ in $G$. By the inductive hypothesis, the number of colorful cells in
  $T'$ is odd. Therefore, $W$ has odd degree in $G$. Since the number of odd
  degree nodes in $G$ is even, then $G$ must contain an odd number of colorful
  cells.
\end{proof}


\section{Relation to Other Results}
Taking the limit of subdivisions of $T$ results in Brouwer's Fixed Point Theorem,
which we state but don't prove.

\begin{theorem}[Brouwer's Fixed Point Theorem]
  Let $S$ be a set in $\mathbb{R}^d$ that is homeomorphic to a closed unit cube.
  Then every continuous function $f:S\to S$ has a fixed point.
\end{theorem}

This result is useful when proving existence of equilibria in game theory, for
example, when trying to establish mixed Nash equilibria. However, Brouwer's
theorem is not an algorithmic result. That is, Brouwer tells you that under
the theorem's assumptions, $f$ has a fixed point, but it does not tell you how
to find it.  It turns out that this class of problem is believed to be computationally hard
(specifically PPAD-complete [not defined in the lecture]), but the evidence of hardness is not as strong as that of NP-hard problems. There are also simplifications available that
lead to approximate equilibria, which may be just as useful, and much easier to
obtain, than exact equilibria.
